# RC Car Maintenance Checklist: A 5-Minute Post-Run Routine

## Intro
Most RC maintenance problems start as small stuff.

Dirt builds up. A bearing gets rough. A screw backs out. A shock starts leaking just enough to ignore. Then one day the car feels terrible, and you are trying to figure out what happened.

A quick post-run routine catches most of that before it turns into broken parts and lost drive time.

---

## The Core Idea (In Plain English)
Good RC maintenance is not about tearing the whole car apart after every battery pack.

It is about doing the same simple checks often enough that small issues do not turn into bigger failures. A five-minute routine keeps the car cleaner, smoother, and easier to trust the next time you set it down.

That means checking the parts that take the most abuse, paying attention to how the car sounds and feels, and replacing wear items before they start taking other parts with them. If you want the broader why behind it, [How to Maintain Your RC Car](/gearheads-gazette/rc-car-maintenance) covers the bigger picture.

Consistency matters more than perfection here. Quick maintenance done often beats a giant cleanup after the car already feels rough.

---

## When This Matters (Use Cases)
### Scenario 1
- You bash in dirt, grass, gravel, or wet conditions
- Debris and moisture build up fast and start wearing parts sooner than you think

### Scenario 2
- You run upgraded power systems or larger batteries
- More speed and load usually mean wear shows up faster

### Scenario 3
- The car feels fine now, but you want it to stay that way
- A short routine is a lot easier than fixing a pile of preventable failures later

---

## What to Look For / Key Considerations
- Brush off loose dirt before it gets packed into moving parts
- Check wheel nuts, motor screws, and suspension hardware
- Spin the drivetrain by hand and listen for rough bearings
- Look for leaking shocks, bent shafts, or extra slop
- Check gears for wear, chipped teeth, or bad mesh
- Pay attention to motor and ESC temps after harder runs
- Replace small worn parts before they start damaging bigger ones

---

## Common Mistakes
- Waiting until the car feels terrible before checking anything
- Assuming a noisy drivetrain is just normal wear
- Skipping post-run checks after a hard crash
- Cleaning the car but ignoring slop, heat, and loose hardware
- Letting one worn part take out two or three others

---

## Real Talk
The best maintenance routine is the one you will actually do.

It does not need to be impressive. It just needs to be consistent. Five minutes with a brush, a quick spin of the drivetrain, and a look over the usual problem spots will catch more than most people expect.

A car that stays clean enough, tight enough, and smooth enough is a lot more fun than one that always feels one run away from something dumb breaking.
