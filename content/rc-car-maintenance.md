# How to Maintain Your RC Car Without Turning It Into a Full-Time Job

## Intro
RC cars don't usually fail all at once.

It's the small stuff. Dirt packed into the drivetrain. A dry bearing. A loose wheel nut. A shock that started leaking two runs ago and got ignored.

If your rig feels slower, louder, rougher, or just off, basic maintenance is usually the reason.

---

## The Core Idea (In Plain English)
RC maintenance is not about making your car look perfect on a shelf. It's about keeping moving parts clean, protected, and working the way they should.

Every run adds wear:
- Dust works into bearings
- Heat breaks things down
- Hard landings loosen hardware
- Water and mud speed all of it up

A simple routine goes a long way:
- Clean the car after runs
- Lubricate what actually needs it
- Inspect high-stress parts
- Fix small problems early

You don't need a full teardown every time. You just need to stay ahead of the obvious trouble. And if you're already sorting out the rest of your setup, it helps to understand how supporting parts affect wear too, especially your [tires](/blogs/news/choosing-the-right-rc-tires), [battery setup](/blogs/news/lipo-batteries-101), and the heat side of things in our [motor temperature guide](/blogs/news/rc-motor-temperature-guide).

---

## When This Matters (Use Cases)

### After a dusty parking lot bash session
The car still runs, but everything feels gritty. Steering slows down. The drivetrain sounds louder than usual.

That's dirt getting into places it shouldn't be.

### After a muddy or wet run
Mud looks cool, but moisture and grime are brutal on bearings, hinges, screws, and electronics if you let it sit overnight.

### After adding more power
More speed means more stress.

Gears, bearings, tires, and driveline parts all take a bigger hit when you step up voltage or drop in a hotter motor.

---

## What to Look For / Key Considerations
- Brush off loose dirt before it gets packed into moving parts
- Check wheel nuts, motor screws, and suspension hardware
- Spin the drivetrain by hand to catch rough bearings early
- Inspect spur and pinion gear for wear or bad mesh
- Check shocks for leaks, bent shafts, or weak damping
- Look for cracks or slop in arms, hubs, and rod ends
- Keep motor and ESC cooling areas clean
- Use light oil on bearings only when needed
- Use grease only where it's intended
- Keep lubricants away from tires and electronics

---

## Common Mistakes
- Blasting the whole car with water and calling it maintenance
- Over-oiling until dirt sticks everywhere
- Ignoring weird noises because "it still runs"
- Skipping checks after a hard crash
- Letting loose hardware turn into bigger failures
- Waiting for bearings to fail instead of replacing them early
- Treating cleaning like it's cosmetic when it actually prevents wear

---

## Real Talk
Most people either do too little maintenance or way too much.

You don't need a full rebuild after every pack. But you also can't run hard every weekend and expect everything to stay tight on its own.

The sweet spot is consistency.

A five-to-ten-minute check after each run beats a full teardown once your car already sounds like a coffee grinder.

The goal isn't perfection.
The goal is a rig that's ready when you are.
