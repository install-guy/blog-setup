# RC Gear Mesh Explained

## Intro
Gear mesh is one of those small setup details that can quietly wreck a good day.

Too tight, and the drivetrain drags, builds heat, and feels loaded up. Too loose, and things get noisy, sloppy, and expensive fast.

The good news is gear mesh is not hard to understand once you know what you are looking for.

---

## The Core Idea (In Plain English)
Gear mesh is just how the pinion gear and spur gear sit against each other.

You want them close enough to transfer power cleanly, but not so tight that they bind. Good mesh feels smooth with a little bit of play. Bad mesh usually shows up as noise, extra heat, roughness, or gears wearing faster than they should.

This matters more than people think because mesh affects efficiency, motor load, drivetrain feel, and long-term reliability. If you are already chasing heat, the [RC Motor Temperature Guide](/gearheads-gazette/rc-motor-temperature-guide) connects right to this.

When mesh is right, the drivetrain disappears into the drive. When it is wrong, the whole setup starts feeling stressed.

---

## When This Matters (Use Cases)
### Scenario 1
- You just swapped a motor or changed gearing
- Any time the motor position changes, mesh needs to be checked again

### Scenario 2
- The car suddenly sounds louder or rougher than it used to
- Bad mesh often shows up in noise before it shows up as broken teeth

### Scenario 3
- You are chewing through spur gears or seeing temps climb for no obvious reason
- Mesh is one of the first simple things worth checking

---

## What to Look For / Key Considerations
- Set mesh with the vehicle powered off and easy to rotate by hand
- Aim for slight free play instead of a jammed-together feel
- Rotate the spur through a full turn before calling it good
- Recheck mesh after a hard hit or crash
- Look at both gears for uneven wear or damaged teeth
- Pay attention to sound on the first easy run after any adjustment

---

## Common Mistakes
- Setting the gears too tight because it feels safer
- Leaving them too loose because they spin freely on the bench
- Checking only one spot on the spur gear
- Swapping motors and forgetting to reset mesh
- Ignoring gear noise until something strips

---

## Real Talk
Most gear mesh problems are not complicated. They are just easy to overlook.

Take two extra minutes, set it right, and a lot of drivetrain problems never happen. A clean, quiet setup almost always starts with the basics being handled properly.

It is one of the least flashy jobs on the bench, but it pays off every time you pull the trigger.
