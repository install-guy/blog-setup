# How to Choose RC Servos

## Intro
Servos usually get ignored until the steering feels weak, slow, or just not up to the job.

Then the questions start fast. How much torque do you need? Does metal gear matter? Is waterproofing worth it? Will the servo even fit?

The right servo upgrade makes a rig feel tighter and more trustworthy. The wrong one usually just creates more guesswork.

---

## The Core Idea (In Plain English)
A servo controls steering, throttle, or other movement depending on the vehicle, but for most RC setups the big conversation is steering feel.

Torque is what gives the servo strength. Speed is how quickly it moves. Gear material matters for durability. Waterproofing matters if the car sees wet, muddy, or unpredictable conditions.

That is why servo choice should match the vehicle, tire size, weight, and how hard the setup gets used. If you are already tightening up the handling side of the car, [RC Suspension Basics](/gearheads-gazette/rc-suspension-basics) helps put servo changes into context.

More servo is not always better, but too little servo usually shows up fast once the tires get bigger or the terrain gets rougher.

---

## When This Matters (Use Cases)
### Scenario 1
- Your steering feels slow or weak when the tires are loaded up
- That usually means the stock servo is running out of strength

### Scenario 2
- You upgraded wheels, tires, or overall vehicle weight
- Bigger loads usually ask more from the steering system

### Scenario 3
- You drive in water, mud, or messy conditions
- Waterproofing starts making a lot more sense once the environment gets less friendly

---

## What to Look For / Key Considerations
- Choose enough torque for the vehicle size and tire load
- Pay attention to servo speed if quick steering response matters
- Pick metal gears when durability matters more than saving a little money
- Check waterproofing if the car sees wet or muddy use
- Confirm size and mounting fit before buying
- Match the upgrade to the rest of the steering system, not just the servo alone

---

## Common Mistakes
- Buying based on the biggest number without checking fitment
- Ignoring servo size and spline compatibility
- Upgrading tires and leaving the steering system underpowered
- Assuming metal gears fix every steering problem
- Forgetting that a sloppy linkage can still make a good servo feel bad

---

## Real Talk
A good servo upgrade is one of those changes you feel right away.

The car tracks cleaner, reacts quicker, and stops feeling like it is arguing with you every time the front tires load up. But the best servo on the shelf still needs to fit the build and the way you drive it.

Buy for the real job the servo has to do, not the one-line spec sheet flex.
