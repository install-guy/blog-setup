# Why Your RC Slides Too Much

## Intro
If your RC keeps pushing wide, breaking loose, or sliding more than it should, it is usually not just bad luck.

Most of the time, the car is telling you something about the tires, the surface, or the setup.

The fix is not always more power or more parts. Usually it starts with figuring out why the car is losing grip in the first place.

---

## The Core Idea (In Plain English)
An RC slides too much when the grip available does not match what the setup is asking the tires to do.

Sometimes that is the wrong tread. Sometimes it is the wrong compound. Sometimes the surface changed. And sometimes the car is just set up too stiff, too loose, or too aggressive for where you are driving.

That is why tire choice usually comes first. If you have not sorted that out yet, [Choosing the Right RC Tires](/gearheads-gazette/choosing-the-right-rc-tires) is the best place to begin.

Grip comes from the whole combination of tire, terrain, suspension, and driving style. Fix the mismatch there, and the car usually gets easier to drive fast.

---

## When This Matters (Use Cases)
### Scenario 1
- You are bashing on dirt, gravel, or mixed backyard surfaces
- One tire setup rarely works perfectly everywhere

### Scenario 2
- The car used to feel planted, but now it is sliding more than normal
- Worn tires, changed conditions, or sloppier suspension may be part of the problem

### Scenario 3
- You added power and now traction feels worse instead of better
- More power without matching the rest of the setup often just makes wheelspin easier

---

## What to Look For / Key Considerations
- Match tire tread and compound to the actual surface
- Check tire wear before blaming the rest of the setup
- Pay attention to suspension slop, ride height, and shock condition
- Be honest about whether the surface has less grip than usual
- Remember that more power often makes grip problems more obvious
- Tune the car for the way you drive, not just the way you want it to look

---

## Common Mistakes
- Running one tire setup on every surface
- Blaming the driver before checking the tires
- Adding power when the chassis still cannot use the grip it has
- Ignoring worn inserts, worn tread, or sloppy suspension parts
- Expecting the car to feel planted on a surface it was never set up for

---

## Real Talk
Most sliding problems are setup problems, not mystery problems.

The car is usually not broken. It is just mismatched. Wrong tire, wrong surface, wrong compound, worn parts, or a setup that got pushed past what it can use.

Once you stop chasing random fixes and start matching the setup to the conditions, the car usually settles down fast.
